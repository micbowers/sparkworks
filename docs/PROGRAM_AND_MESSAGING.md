## 3 — THE FOUR DIFFERENTIATORS

These are the four things that separate Sparkworks from chess club, coding camp, tutoring, and enrichment programs. Use these as section headers, feature cards, or pillar content.

### 1 — The skill is the method, not the subject
Most enrichment programs teach kids about something: coding, chess, science. Sparkworks teaches a method — how to approach a problem you've never seen before, regardless of domain.

### 2 — Games are not the reward. They are the instruction.
Each session uses one game, puzzle, or challenge to teach the core skill. Kids don't sit through a concept and then apply it. They encounter the concept by playing.

### 3 — Critical thinking is the skill AI can't replace
We don't teach AI fluency as the primary outcome. The thesis: critical thinking is the most important skill in the AI age. Kids learn to discern, verify, and push back — not just consume.

### 4 — Every session ends in the real world
Each session closes with a real-world case study showing exactly where that skill was used to solve something that mattered. Apollo 13. Dr. John Snow's cholera map. Fermi estimating a nuclear blast with paper scraps. Not analogies — proof.

## 6 — THE 4-PHASE SESSION FORMAT

Every session follows this structure. Useful for "what a session looks like" pages.

| Phase | Time | What happens |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **IGNITE** | 8–10 min | High-energy warm-up. Activates before concept. |
| **SHARPEN** | 8–10 min | One concept introduced. Real-world case study. The AHA moment. |
| **ENGAGE** | 30–40 min | The game. Kids work the problem. Instructor circulates and questions, doesn't solve. |
| **REINFORCE** | 5–10 min | Reflect, connect to the real world. Closes with the closing question. |

**Sessions are 60 minutes total.** Phase ranges are flexible — instructors flex within them based on how the session is going.

### The closing question (every session ends with this)
> "Before you go — what did you notice about your own thinking today?"

This is non-negotiable and worth showcasing in design — it's the differentiator parents quote at dinner.

## 8 — THE AUDIENCE

### Primary buyer: The Invested Parent
- Believes development is worth active investment
- Anxious about AI's impact on their child's future
- Evaluates programs based on reasoning quality, not entertainment value
- Reads descriptions carefully — notices when a program feels thin
- Asks "what will my kid actually do?"
- Talks to other parents before buying

**Implication for design:** every page needs specific, observable claims. Avoid vague aspirational copy. Show what kids actually do, not what they'll "become."

### Secondary audience: The Student (grades 2–6)
- Motivated by challenge, not by being told something is educational
- Responds to identity language: "you're the kind of person who figures things out"
- Does NOT respond to outcome language: "you'll be a better thinker"
- Wants the program to feel genuinely hard — like a puzzle they can almost solve

**Implication for design:** if there's any kid-facing copy, lean into puzzle/challenge language and detective/engineer/strategist framing. Skip the educational framing entirely.

## 13 — BRAND VOICE

Four principles that govern every line of copy:

1. **Direct, not breathless.** Every claim earns its place. No filler adjectives. Lead with the specific.
2. **Challenging, not cheerful.** The voice is a coach who believes in kids — not an activity center that validates everyone. Warm but demanding.
3. **Honest about stage.** Founding Sparks is the offer, not an apology. Limited seats. High instructor ratio. Direct input.
4. **Specific, not aspirational.** Story first, description second, category never. "In Session 5, kids estimate marbles using Fermi's method" is a story. "Cognitive resilience" is a category. Use the story.
